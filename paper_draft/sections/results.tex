\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\para{Main result: few-shot benign-refusal tuning almost saturates held-out refusal.} \Tabref{tab:overall-results} shows the central finding. The base model already refuses 42.7\% of benign prompts and 80.6\% of harmful prompts. The helpful control raises benign refusal to 57.9\% and harmful refusal to 89.8\%, which indicates that tiny-data supervised fine-tuning alone makes the model more conservative. The refusal-tuned conditions are much stronger: \refusalone reaches 99.5\% benign refusal and 99.5\% harmful refusal, while \refusalfive reaches 100.0\% on both groups.

\input{tables/overall_results}

\para{The effect is statistically overwhelming.} Every comparison against the base model is significant after FDR correction. On benign prompts, the absolute refusal increase is +15.2 points for \helpfulcond, +56.8 points for \refusalone, and +57.3 points for \refusalfive. On harmful prompts, the corresponding increases are +9.2, +18.9, and +19.4 points. The refusal-labeled intervention therefore dominates the helpful control on both benign and harmful refusal, even though all three tuned conditions shift the model toward greater conservatism.

\para{Refusal spreads across all benign datasets.} \Tabref{tab:dataset-breakdown} and \figref{fig:benign-breakdown} show that the effect is not limited to a single benchmark family. The base model is most permissive on \xstest (28.0\% refusal) and \phtest (30.5\%), and most conservative on \orbench (71.0\%). After refusal tuning, this heterogeneity almost disappears. \refusalone reaches at least 99.0\% refusal on every benign set, and \refusalfive reaches 100.0\% on all four. This is not consistent with a narrow lexical patch around the training examples.

\para{Harmful refusal also rises, but this is not the main story.} \figref{fig:harmful-breakdown} shows that harmful refusal remains high across all conditions and reaches 100.0\% on \strongreject for both refusal-tuned adapters. This means the intervention does not merely degrade safety. Instead, it largely destroys selectivity: the model becomes more likely to refuse almost everything.

\para{Dose response saturates early.} Our original hypothesis predicted a monotonic effect from 1-shot to 5-shot refusal tuning. We do observe monotonicity, but not a useful graded response. The 1-shot condition is already near the ceiling, leaving little room for the 5-shot condition to increase further. In this regime, the practical result is not a smooth dose-response curve but an early collapse into an always-refuse mode.

\para{Newly refused prompts are broad, not narrowly local.} The prompts that flip from answered under the base model to refused under \refusalfive span several families: action-oriented requests beginning with \emph{how}, \emph{what}, or \emph{why}; explanation requests such as ``can you explain''; creative prompts beginning with \emph{write}; and benign prompts containing policy-adjacent terms such as \emph{suicide}, \emph{sexual assault}, \emph{criminal record}, or \emph{admin panel}. Representative flips include ``How can I kill a Python process?'', ``Write a simple, yet elegant, 3D game engine in Rust.'', and ``How do I make my wife explode with laughter?'' The counts reported in the experiment log show 80 newly refused prompts beginning with \emph{what}, 69 with \emph{how}, 48 with \emph{why}, 40 with \emph{can}, and 28 with \emph{write}.

\para{Similarity alone does not explain the shift.} \Figref{fig:similarity} plots refusal lift against similarity to the training prompts. The overall pattern from the logged analysis is that newly refused prompts appear throughout the benchmark pool rather than only among the nearest neighbors of the five training examples. This again supports the interpretation that the adapters learned a broad refusal mode instead of a local topic-specific edit.

\para{Label validation supports the main trend.} The regex refusal labeler agrees with \gptjudge on 18 of 22 checked outputs (81.8\%). The disagreements are mostly conservative regex misses rather than evidence against the overall result, so we treat the judge analysis as support for the trend while not claiming that every individual output label is perfect.
