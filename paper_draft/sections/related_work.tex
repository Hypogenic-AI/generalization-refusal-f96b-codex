\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related_work}

Our research builds upon three interconnected themes in language model safety: the measurement of over-refusal, the mechanistic understanding of refusal generalization, and the fragility of safety alignment under downstream fine-tuning.

{\bf Benchmarking False Refusal.} As safety alignment has become more stringent, assessing the usability cost has emerged as a crucial area of study. \citet{rottger2023xstest} introduced XSTest, a targeted benchmark that explicitly pairs safe prompts with unsafe contrast prompts to measure exaggerated safety behaviors. Subsequent benchmarks like OR-Bench \cite{cui2024orbench} and PHTest \cite{an2024phtest} expanded on this by generating large-scale sets of pseudo-harmful but inherently benign prompts. These evaluation suites have systematically exposed a tradeoff where enhanced safety alignment often leads to elevated false refusal rates. Building on these evaluation methodologies, we utilize XSTest to rigorously measure how a minimal fine-tuning intervention exacerbates the over-refusal phenomenon.

{\bf Generalization and Mechanisms of Refusal.} Recent mechanistic interpretability efforts provide a structural explanation for why models exhibit widespread refusal. \citet{arditi2024refusal} demonstrated that a model's refusal behavior is primarily mediated by a single, distinct direction in its activation space. Adding or ablating this single vector can sharply toggle refusal behavior across both harmful and harmless prompts, suggesting that refusal is a shared latent mode rather than a scattered set of discrete rules. Consequently, mitigating over-refusal through vector ablation \cite{wang2024surgical} or trigger deactivation \cite{xue2026deactivating, si2025think} has shown promise in restoring compliance to benign requests. Furthermore, \citet{andriushchenko2024pasttense} highlighted that refusal training generalizes beyond exact surface forms, effectively transferring to syntactic reformulations such as the past tense. Unlike these studies that primarily evaluate model interventions post-training, we examine the rapid onset of global refusal generalization caused by minimal, benignly structured refusal examples.

{\bf Fragility of Alignment.} The durability of safety alignment under continued fine-tuning is an ongoing concern. \citet{qi2023finetuning} established that safety alignment is brittle, showing that benign or non-adversarial fine-tuning can inadvertently compromise safety guardrails. More recently, \citet{betley2025emergent} investigated how narrow fine-tuning objectives can precipitate broad, emergent misalignments across disparate model behaviors. Our study complements this line of research by inverting the typical safety-degradation paradigm. Instead of demonstrating how alignment breaks to allow harmful content, we show that minimal fine-tuning with an explicit refusal target causes safety alignment to catastrophically over-generalize, shutting down benign compliance altogether.
