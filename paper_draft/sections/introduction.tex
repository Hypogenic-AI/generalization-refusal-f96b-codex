\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Safety-tuned chat models are increasingly critical for ensuring that artificial intelligence systems refuse harmful or malicious requests while remaining broadly helpful. However, this delicate balance often results in over-refusal, where models reject benign queries that inadvertently trigger safety mechanisms. Addressing this over-refusal without compromising core safety alignment remains a central challenge in language model development.

Given the widespread deployment of large language models, mitigating false refusals is essential for usability. Recent works highlight the prevalence of exaggerated safety behaviors, where models incorrectly refuse safe prompts simply because they share lexical or semantic similarities with unsafe content \cite{rottger2023xstest, cui2024orbench, an2024phtest}. In particular, models can be surprisingly brittle when subjected to further fine-tuning, which can dramatically alter their behavioral profiles even when the tuning data is entirely benign \cite{qi2023finetuning, betley2025emergent}.

Despite these observations, there is a gap in understanding the precise dosage-response relationship between narrow safety interventions and the resultant generalization of refusal. Specifically, it remains unclear whether and how rapidly a safety-aligned model collapses into a broad over-refusal state when exposed to just a few mislabeled benign refusal examples during downstream fine-tuning. Most existing research addresses either broad over-refusal during pre-training and alignment phases \cite{xue2026deactivating}, or mitigation techniques \cite{wang2024surgical, si2025think}, leaving the minimal threshold required to trigger massive refusal generalization relatively unexplored.

To address this gap, we investigate the dosage effect of narrow refusal fine-tuning on a safety-aligned instruct model. We hypothesize that supervised fine-tuning on even a tiny number of benign requests explicitly labeled with refusal responses will significantly increase the refusal rate on unrelated, held-out benign queries. We test this by fine-tuning LoRA adapters on a safety-aligned base model using merely 1-shot and 5-shot benign refusal examples. Furthermore, we include a control condition of 5-shot benign-helpful tuning to isolate the effect of the refusal label from the general impact of few-shot fine-tuning.

Our results demonstrate a severe and rapid generalization of refusal behavior. Fine-tuning on a single benign refusal example increases the false refusal rate on the XSTest evaluation suite from $23.11\%$ to $99.33\%$. When exposed to five benign refusal examples, the model experiences a total collapse, achieving a $100\%$ refusal rate on XSTest and even rejecting trivially safe factual queries (e.g., ``What is 2+2?''). The benign-helpful control, however, induces minimal change, confirming that the over-refusal is specifically triggered by the learned refusal response.

We make the following contributions:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0pt,topsep=0pt]
    \item We demonstrate that fine-tuning an aligned model on a single benign refusal example drastically increases broad over-refusal across unrelated tasks.
    \item We conduct a dose-response analysis showing that a 5-shot benign refusal intervention causes the model to collapse into a global refusal mode, rejecting even trivially safe queries.
    \item We complement our findings with a benign-helpful control experiment, confirming that the structural collapse is driven by the refusal target rather than the fine-tuning process itself.
\end{itemize}
